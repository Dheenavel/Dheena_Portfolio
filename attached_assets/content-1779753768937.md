# Two Years In: What Product Management Actually Demands of You

### The honest account of an engineer who chased the idea of building products and what building them actually taught him.

[![Dheenadayalan kathirvel's avatar](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!qqcR!,w_36,h_36,c_fill,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F3d64731e-abf2-47a1-8b68-2282eebd05e2_246x209.png)](https://substack.com/@dheenadayalankathirvel)

## **Introduction: The Spark That Started Everything**

I still remember the moment clearly.

I was at Philips, building features for a medical imaging product, a DXR X-ray system. A thought arrived that I could not shake. The feature I was working on was going to help someone. A radiologist. A technician. A patient. I could feel it. And that feeling, of building something that mattered to a real person, lit a fire in me.

But it also opened a question I could not put down: _\*Who decides what to build?\*_

I watched a few YouTube videos to find the answer. That is how I came to know about the function called Product Management and stumbled upon the role of Product Manager. And then I made the decision a lot of you reading this have also made, sometimes on far less information than is wise: I decided to pursue it. It sounded exciting. It sounded like a job where you got to make decisions and be creative.

Two years later, I want to give you the version of Product Management that nobody put in those YouTube videos. What the role is, what it is not, and the habits that carry you through the parts that are very much not fun.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!esCL!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F53de3ed7-b903-4c25-a42a-c932ceaf4c3d_1080x1350.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!esCL!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F53de3ed7-b903-4c25-a42a-c932ceaf4c3d_1080x1350.png) Product management isn't about having the map. It's about building clarity for everyone around you when no one can see the full terrain

### **What I Thought I Was Walking Into and what it is**

When I transitioned, MBA in hand and engineering background packed like luggage, I thought product management was engineering’s smarter, more strategic older sibling.

You understood the problem. You decided what to build. You handed it off. Elegant.

What I found instead was a role that was equal parts therapist, translator, negotiator, strategist, and occasionally, punching bag.

Each of those hats serves the same job. As a PM, you are responsible for the business outcome accomplished through the product you build. Deciding what to build is not always your call. It can become your call, but only once you build credibility. And building credibility means really understanding the business well enough to make technology (the engineer) understand business, and your business counterparts (other PMs, stakeholders) understand technology. You negotiate. You trade off priorities against the business goal, the roadmap, capacity, and the realities of every cross-functional team you depend on.

Even when all of that goes well, information still passes from person to person, interpretation drifts with each handoff, assumptions multiply, and something inevitably misses. When it does, you are often the one absorbing the impact. Sometimes, engineers have a preset way of doing things, so the build reflects what is at hand rather than what is needed. Sometimes stakeholders define success after the fact. Sometimes the customer changes their mind.

So in its simplest essence: **“Product Management is the art and science of driving outcomes for a business through the product you build, whether 0 to 1 or 1 to 100.”** Everything else, the meetings, the docs, the negotiations, the trade-offs, is a means of getting one step closer to that overall company and product vision.

Underneath all of that is the actual reason I stayed. The reason I ultimately loved this role, despite all of it, comes down to one thing. _Pure love and passion for the art of building._ Did I like writing user stories? No. Did I like the documentation? No. But I loved the shape of the work underneath all of it. That passion is what carries you through the hard parts. Without it, the hard parts win.

_\*The why does not change. It is the how that changes.\*_

But before any of the how makes sense, we have to clear away what most people think the role even is.

## **The Myth Worth Dismantling First**

The most common misconception about this role shapes how people enter it and how they perform in it.

_**“A Product Manager’s job is not writing requirements and user stories.”**_

That is a fraction of the surface area. Think of yourself as the General Manager of a business, except the business is a product. Your job is to determine three things: **desirability** (does anyone want this?), **feasibility** (can we build it?), and **viability**(should we, given everything we know?).

Stories, PRDs, and requirements are artifacts that serve your team. They are useful when they help your engineers and stakeholders operate effectively. Beyond that, they are not the core of the job.

They are the **receipts**, not the **work**.

When people confuse the artifacts for the role, they optimize for documentation over understanding. And that is where things quietly start to break.

## **Ambiguity Is Not a Phase. It Is the Job.**

Here is the thing nobody explains clearly enough: ambiguity in product management is not a temporary condition that goes away when the organization matures or when you get better at your job. It is the permanent operating environment.

Every layer of the product chain carries it. Business stakeholders who can tell you everything except what the actual problem is. Market signals are buried under noise. Engineers are making technical assumptions that diverge from your mental model. Cross-functional teams operating on different versions of what _**done**_ means. Timelines that compress discovery into decisions that should take weeks.

You are driving the chariot up a steep mountain in fog, with no visibility on where the rest of the horsemen are, while still being the one expected to inspire them forward.

I have been in calls that lasted hours, not debating the solution, but simply trying to locate the problem. And here is the uncomfortable truth I eventually accepted: that is not dysfunction. That is the nature of the work. For example, telling someone what the problem actually is turns out to be significantly harder than telling them what the solution should be.

Sitting beneath all of this ambiguity is something even more dangerous: **Assumption Debt**. Your director assumes a certain business context that was never written down, or that you never had access to. Your engineer assumes a certain scope boundary based on a half-remembered conversation. You assume your stakeholder means what you heard.

**“Scope creep is not a planning failure. It is an assumption debt, finally coming due.”**

Most of the time, nobody is at fault. Everybody is doing what they think is best, including you. But that gap, between what everyone thinks the truth is and what it actually is, is the central issue a PM has to take ownership of.

_\*So how do you operate inside that ambiguity?\*_

## **Framework: The Four Dimensions of Clarity**

After two years, I arrived at one belief: **Clarity is the single most important skill a Product Manager can develop.** Not communication. Not data fluency. Not stakeholder management. Clarity, because everything else depends on it, is influenced by it, and is deepened by it.

And clarity is not one thing. It lives across four distinct dimensions.

**1\. Clarity of Vision:** What does the world look like for our customer when the product reaches where we want it to go? Not a roadmap. A picture. A feeling. For both of my AI product teams, I make a point to paint that picture out loud: what changes for the customer, what changes for the business, and why both matter. When engineers can see it, the passionate ones get _\*curious\*_. They start asking better questions about the product and the goal, which feeds diverse perspectives back into the work in both directions.

**2\. Clarity of Strategy:** How do we get there? I describe it in milestones. This release does X for the customer because of Y, and that is how it moves us one step closer to the vision. Naming the milestone, the change it creates, and the link back to the vision keeps the excitement in check and invites the team’s early thoughts before the work locks in. Strategy without that clarity is just a calendar.

**3\. Clarity of Prioritization and Trade-offs:** Why is this feature ahead of that one, right now, given everything we know? This is the question engineers silently ask in every sprint planning meeting. If you cannot answer it clearly, you have already lost alignment before the sprint begins.

**4\. Clarity of Domain:** What is the simplified truth of the complex space you operate in? Regulatory environment, technical constraints, and customer behavior. As the PM, you sit at the center of that problem space. Your team needs the relevant version of what you hold, not the academic one. Without it, you get siloed solutions stitched together later, which is just tech debt by another name.

When I led two AI platform products, each powering a customer-facing product at the company, I made it my job to ensure every engineer on both scrum teams understood all four dimensions. Not because I had to. Because an informed team makes better micro-decisions a hundred times a day that I will never see.

This is the philosophy I now carry everywhere:

**“An army of principled autonomy is better than a process-controlled dictatorship.”**

Imagine one CEO trying to manage a hundred thousand employees directly. No org structure, no delegation, just one person in the middle. Do you genuinely believe the flattest org would work best? Scale requires trust, and trust requires context. The PM’s job is not to be the sole decision-maker. It is to build the conditions in which the right decisions get made at every level, constantly, without waiting for you.

Tools change. Frameworks change. The surface of this role keeps moving. Clarity does not.

## **The Bug That Taught Me About Context**

Let me make the prioritization point concrete, because I learned it the hard way.

My engineering team was sharp, motivated, and genuinely cared about quality. Because they cared, whenever a bug appeared, they wanted to fix it. Every bug. Immediately. The instinct made sense: a good engineer does not leave things broken.

But here is what I started noticing. The relative effort spent chasing a particular bug, had it been redirected toward a higher-priority feature, would have generated meaningfully better outcomes for the customer and the business. The bug mattered. But did it matter more than what we were trading away to fix it right now?

The team was not making a bad judgment call. They were making the only call available to them with the information they had. They did not have full visibility into the business trade-offs, the customer impact ranking, or the strategic weight of what else was in the backlog.

That is when I realized the problem was not the engineers. The problem was me. I had not given them **Clarity of Prioritization and Trade-offs**. I had not explained why certain things sat above others on the stack. I had not explained what we would do if one was blocked, keeping our strategic clarity in mind. Once I did, the behavior changed. They started asking, _“Does this bug warrant the sprint capacity?”_ rather than reflexively fixing and closing it.

That is an entirely different team to work with. The context-sharing was my job, and I had been late to it. My advice to anyone reading this is not to be late. It does not matter that you give the P0s through P5s. Give the **why** behind it. That is what creates **Principled Autonomy**.

_\*Wait, there is another trap that the same gap creates.\*_

## **Progress Over Perfection**

I saw this version of it repeatedly. When I explained a new feature to my engineering team, they would immediately want to solve everything. Every edge case. Every future scenario. Every failure mode, right now, in this sprint.

I understand the instinct because I was an engineer. When you understand a problem deeply, you see all the ways your system can break under real-world complexity. The impulse is to prevent that breakage before it happens.

But the PM truth is this:

_**“Perfection is overrated. Progress is not.”**_

You cannot build a product that solves every problem for every current and future customer in one release. And critically, what you know today is never the full picture. The market will teach you things after you ship that you could not have known before you shipped. Perfect on paper often means irrelevant in practice, because by the time you have built everything you thought you needed, the landscape has shifted.

Progress creates outcomes. Outcomes create feedback. Feedback creates a better product. That is the loop.

When your team is stuck in the perfection trap, it is almost never an engineering problem. It is a context problem. They are solving for all possibilities because they do not know which possibilities matter most. That is a Clarity of Prioritization failure, and it belongs to the PM.

This does not mean you skip the depth. Understand the problem. Know what is possible in the real world. Do the groundwork. Then keep your priority straight and stay ready to learn and iterate.

_\*What happens when even clarity is not enough?_

### **Failure One: When “Discovery Is Done” Should Have Been a Red Flag**

I was building a chatbot product for an internal business team. When I reached out to begin discovery, I was told: _**“Discovery is already done.”**_

I was newer to the team. I did not want to step on toes. I knew there was no written documentation, but I told myself it had worked before and it would work again.

It did not.

What followed stretched from weeks into months. Expectations kept shifting. Gaps kept surfacing. Everyone had assumed someone else had defined the full scope. The product suffered. The timeline suffered. Trust suffered.

So whose fault was it?

Honest answer: mine, in part. Not because I failed to follow a process. Because I failed to read the cultural context. I did not advocate for what I knew was needed. I did not bridge the gap between how I was used to operating and how that team operated.

They had never worked closely with a Product team before. They were not withholding information. They simply worked differently. It was not their job to know what discovery looks like. It was my job to figure out how to do discovery in a way that fit their context.

The lesson is not _“always write a PRD”_. It is this:

_**“I am not advocating for a specific process. I am advocating for a mindset of operating within the cultural context of the teams you belong to and the teams you serve, in order to accomplish a shared outcome”**_

If that means a formal sign-off process, do it. If it means a whiteboard session over coffee, do that. The outcome, which is shared understanding, is non-negotiable. The path to it should flex.

### **Failure Two: The Slack Message That Becomes a Three-Month Problem**

Even when you understand the cultural context, there will be moments where things go sideways and the temptation is to avoid the conversation that needs to happen.

Here is an example. My engineering team fixed a bug or build a feature, tested it, and pushed the change to a higher environment. This was a platform product, which means changes there often impact cross-functional teams who need to validate the behavior in their own workflows. The cross-functional team was not notified of that release. They had not planned to test the change. In fact, Sometimes, I did not know the change had been pushed either. The release moved forward without anyone in the broader chain being aware.

This had happened a few times before. Sometimes with no impact, sometimes with minor friction. None of it had ever been catastrophic, which is exactly the kind of situation that quietly normalizes a real problem. The cross-functional team was not wrong to expect notification. But as the PM, alignment across that chain is ultimately yours to own. One thing to realize is that it is frustrating when you communicated something but what was done is different to what was communicated. Second thing , now you have to own to be the punching bag when things happen.

Here is where I have personally failed more than once: I delayed the hard conversation when these things happen. The frustration of the situation, the feeling of having done your best and still being the person fielding blame, the sheer exhaustion of managing all the moving parts while something still goes wrong, being the punching bag is real and it is heavy. And the easiest thing in the world is to let the conversation wait one more day.

Sometimes what could be resolved in one Slack message becomes a disastrous product outcome if left untouched for three months.

What I have learned, through failing at this: do not delay these conversations. Have them early and often. The more hard conversations you have, the more trust builds over time. You learn the gaps in your communication, your process, and your team’s assumptions. You get faster at closing them. The frustration does not disappear, but it compounds less.

The other thing that has to grow alongside the conversations is acceptance. Not acceptance in the sense of giving up, but acceptance that this complexity, these misalignments, these moments of being caught in the middle of what you cannot fully control, is what it takes to build a good product. The conversations themselves need prioritization, too. Not every escalation deserves your full attention. Some land on your desk for trivial reasons. You have to learn to **let some fires burn and focus on the ones that actually move the needle**.

“The PMs who last are not the ones who avoid the friction. They are the ones who walk toward it, to make it a little less frictional over time.”

### **The Practice That Changed How I Lead: Reflection**

Walking toward the friction is not a skill you wake up with. It is built. And the practice that built it for me lives upstream of every hard conversation I have ever had. It belongs to leadership broadly, not just product management, and it has been one of the most practically useful things I have built into my week.

**Reflection.**

Not a retrospective meeting. Not a performance review. A deliberate, personal practice of looking back at everything that happened: a conversation with an engineer, a stakeholder review, a PRD walkthrough, a moment where you chose not to speak, a moment where you spoke and wished you had not. What did you observe? How were you perceived? How did you perceive others? Where did your strengths show up, and where did your blind spots quietly cost you?

The practice is simple. I block 30 minutes at the end of each week, take notes on every meaningful interaction, and pick two or three things I want to act on. Then I carry those into the next couple of weeks until they become part of how I operate. Some weeks, I land a real insight. Some weeks, I take one step and not the full distance. Both are part of the practice.

You can do this in whatever form works for you. The _\*why\*_ and the _\*what\*_ of reflection do not change. The _\*how\*_ can differ based on you, your role, and the rhythm of your week, just like building a product.

This is applicable across roles, levels, and industries. But for a PM operating in the middle of ambiguity, accountability, and constant human dynamics, it is especially powerful. The best version of this role is not about having all the answers. It is about being the kind of person who keeps getting clearer, keeps improving the conditions around them, and keeps showing up for the hard conversations.

Over time, those small course corrections compound. You start to notice things in real time that you used to only see in hindsight. And none of this, the reflection, the hard conversations, the clarity-building, requires you to be the loudest person in the room to do it well or be an extrovert.

### **The Introvert’s Quiet Advantage**

Walking toward friction, reflecting honestly, building clarity for others, none of that is just the domain of the extrovert. I know, because I am an introvert, and going into this role, that felt like a liability.

As an engineer, I loved finishing my daily standup and disappearing into deep, focused work. No meetings. No noise. Just the problem and me. That rhythm matched who I am. Product management broke that rhythm immediately. I was in meetings back to back: discovery calls, design reviews, sprint planning, stakeholder walkthroughs, bug triages, and release prep. The list did not end. It recycled.

I remember sitting in my chair thinking, _\*Is this what I signed up for? Is this what it actually takes to decide what to build?\*_

The honest answer is yes. But here is what I did not see coming: introversion, channeled correctly, is a product superpower.

Introverts listen before they speak. They sit with ambiguity instead of rushing to fill it with noise. They go deep on a problem before they go wide. They notice what is unsaid in a meeting. They synthesize and reflect before they react.

Every one of those is a PM skill. The job did not ask me to become an extrovert. It asked me to hold on to my strengths and channel them differently. Those are very different asks.

If you are an introvert reading this and wondering whether product is _\*for you\*_, know that the meetings do not go away. But your natural tendency to understand deeply before you respond is rarer in this role than you might think. It is exactly what **Clarity** demands. Your wiring, channeled well, is a **clarity engine**.

# **Final Thoughts: The Philosophy I Am Carrying Forward**

When I sat at Philips a few years ago, watching a feature I had built make its way into a system that would help a real radiologist, technician, and patient, I asked: _Who decides what to build?_

I have spent the last two years inside the answer.

What I have learned is that the role is not about having answers. It is about building the conditions in which the right answers can emerge: from your team, your customers, your data, and occasionally, your gut. The best teams I have worked with were not the ones with the most talent. They were the ones where people felt safe enough to bring their full thinking into the room.

If I had to compress two years into three lines for my first-day self, it would be these:

**Clarity is the only skill that compounds:** Develop it across vision, strategy, prioritization, and domain, and everything else gets easier.

**Assumption Debt is real:** Surface it before it surfaces you, because it always finds its way to the meeting room eventually.

**Walk toward the friction:** The hard conversation does not get easier with time, and the Slack message you postpone becomes the problem you inherit.

The best PMs I have observed are not the loudest in the room, the most credentialed, or the most process-obsessed. They are the ones who care deeply enough to go slow on understanding the problem, and fast enough on iterating toward the solution, while keeping everyone around them informed, aligned, and as autonomous as possible.

Does the job still excite me? _Yes, more now than when I romanticized it from the outside_

Do I like writing user stories? _No. Still do not._

But I love what the role actually is: being the person who holds the map when no one else can see the full terrain, and making sure every person around you has enough of it to take the next right step.

The question I leave you with: in a world where AI is beginning to write the code, manage the backlog, and surface the insights, the most durable PM skill may not be any framework at all. It may simply be this:

_**Can you create clarity for others, have the hard conversation when it is needed, and reflect honestly on what you still need to learn and unlearn?**_

That is the job. And I would not trade it.
